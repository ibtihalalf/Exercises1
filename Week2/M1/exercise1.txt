John Snow
Question 1: Correlation vs. Causation
The map showed a high concentration of deaths near the Broad Street pump. However, proximity alone only proves a correlation. What specific steps did Snow take to move his argument from correlation (deaths happen near the pump) to causation (the pump water is causing the deaths)?
Question 2: The Danger of Missing Data
Imagine if Snow had only collected data from the local hospitals, rather than walking the streets and knocking on doors. How would this have changed his dataset, and how might it have altered his conclusions?
Question 3: The “Miasma” of Today
The “miasma theory” was a widely accepted consensus that was fundamentally incorrect but seemed to explain the surface-level facts (Soho smelled bad, and people in Soho were dying). What is a modern example of a “miasma”—a widely accepted assumption in business, society, or science that might fall apart if rigorously tested with data?

Answer 1: 
John Snow moved from correlation to causation by:
	•	Collecting individual exposure data (who used the pump)
	•	Analyzing exceptions (e.g., brewery workers didn’t get sick; a distant victim drank pump water)
	•	Comparing exposed vs. non-exposed groups
	•	Performing an intervention (removing the pump handle → cases dropped)

Answer 2: 
If John Snow had only used hospital data:
	•	His dataset would be incomplete and biased (missing people who died at home or never went to hospital).
	•	He would lose key details like water sources and individual behaviors.
	•	Important exceptions (e.g., brewery workers, distant cases) might be missed.
 Result: He might have failed to link cholera to the pump, weakening or even misinterpreting the cause.
Answer 3: 
More data automatically leads to better decisions.
